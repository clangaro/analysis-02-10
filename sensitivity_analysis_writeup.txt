SENSITIVITY ANALYSIS: SENSOR QUALITY AND CIRCADIAN METRIC ROBUSTNESS
=====================================================================
For inclusion in Methods and/or Supplementary Materials


METHODS SECTION TEXT
--------------------

Sensitivity analysis: sensor quality

To assess whether inter-sensor variability in the passive infrared (PIR)
monitors influenced circadian metrics, we performed a three-part
sensitivity analysis.

First, we recomputed all circadian metrics (IS, IV, RA, amplitude, MESOR,
phase) from the raw 1-minute PIR time-series using 60-minute epoch bins to
match ClockLab defaults. Non-parametric metrics (IS, IV, RA) were computed
following Van Someren et al. (1999): interdaily stability (IS) and
intradaily variability (IV) were calculated on the full flattened
time-series, and relative amplitude (RA) was derived from M10 and L5
values computed on the mean 24-hour activity profile using circular
rolling windows. Cosinor parameters (MESOR, amplitude, acrophase) were
obtained by fitting y = MESOR + a*cos(wt) + b*sin(wt) (w = 2pi/24 h)
via ordinary least squares, with phase referenced to midnight for
cross-condition comparability. Per-mouse PRE/POST windows were defined
using each cohort's documented light-treatment start date.

Second, we applied within-mouse z-score normalisation to the raw activity
counts prior to metric computation. Because IS and IV are computed as
ratios of variance components, they are algebraically scale-invariant:
multiplying all activity counts by a constant (as would occur with
differing sensor gains) cancels in both the numerator and denominator.
Consistent with this, z-score normalisation produced negligible changes
in IS (raw PRE mean = 0.402, normalised = 0.403), IV (raw = 1.079,
normalised = 1.072), and delta_IS (raw mean = -0.097, normalised =
-0.088).

Third, we identified mice with potential sensor artefacts by flagging
recordings with consecutive zero-activity runs exceeding 6 hours or
overall zero-activity exceeding 80% of epochs. This identified 51 of
84 mice with raw data files as having at least one flagged recording
period. We then re-ran all primary statistical models excluding flagged
mice (clean n = 50 for circadian, n = 48-51 for behavioural outcomes)
and compared results to the full-sample analysis.


RESULTS SECTION TEXT
--------------------

Sensitivity analysis: sensor exclusion

Excluding 51 sensor-flagged mice did not alter any primary conclusions
(Table S_). All circadian PRE-POST x Light interaction terms remained
non-significant: IS (full sample: beta = 0.002, p = 0.958; clean sample:
beta = 0.039, p = 0.345), IV (full: beta = 0.079, p = 0.196; clean:
beta = 0.062, p = 0.465), RA (full: beta = -0.017, p = 0.612; clean:
beta = -0.016, p = 0.730), and amplitude (full: beta = 0.168, p = 0.463;
clean: beta = 0.226, p = 0.398). Effect directions were consistent across
samples for all metrics.

For behavioural outcomes, the light treatment effect on Barnes maze Trial 6
accuracy remained non-significant (full: Fisher's exact p = 1.000; clean:
p = 0.479). Note that Barnes maze accuracy was at floor in both samples,
with 49 of 50 clean-sample mice scoring zero correct entries at Trial 6,
precluding parametric modelling (binomial GLM exhibited perfect
separation). NOR discrimination index showed no light effect in either
sample (full: beta = -0.052, p = 0.394; clean: beta = -0.044, p = 0.613).

Bootstrap mediation analyses (Light -> delta_IS -> behaviour) yielded
indirect effects with 95% confidence intervals spanning zero in both
samples: Barnes (full: -0.028, CI [-0.141, 0.061]; clean: -0.104,
CI [-0.549, 0.023]) and NOR (full: 0.002, CI [-0.011, 0.020]; clean:
0.000, CI [-0.041, 0.048]).

The scale-invariance of IS and IV to sensor gain differences, combined
with the consistency of results across full and sensor-screened samples,
indicates that inter-sensor variability did not drive the null findings
for circadian or behavioural outcomes.

NOTES FOR DISCUSSION
--------------------

1. The high proportion of sensor-flagged mice (51/84 = 61%) warrants
   acknowledgement as a limitation. However, because the primary circadian
   metrics (IS, IV) are ratio-based and therefore inherently insensitive to
   sensor gain, the impact on conclusions is minimal.

2. The Barnes maze floor effect (near-zero accuracy at Trial 6 in both
   groups) is a separate methodological concern unrelated to sensor quality.
   This suggests that Trial 6 may not provide sufficient sensitivity to
   detect treatment effects, and future studies might benefit from extended
   training or alternative endpoints (e.g., latency, learning curve slope
   across trials).

3. The z-score normalisation result provides a formal demonstration that
   IS and IV are scale-invariant, which can be stated as a mathematical
   property rather than an empirical finding: for any constant c > 0,
   IS(cx) = IS(x) and IV(cx) = IV(x), since c^2 cancels in the
   numerator and denominator of both formulas.

4. RA is not fully scale-invariant (it depends on the ratio M10/L5 of
   absolute activity levels in the most/least active windows), but RA
   showed no treatment effect in either sample, so this distinction does
   not affect conclusions.
